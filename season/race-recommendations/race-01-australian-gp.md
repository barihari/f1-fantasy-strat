## Circuit Analysis

### Track Characteristics
- **Length:** 5.278km, 58 laps
- **Overtake Zones:** Turn 1 braking zone, Turn 3 DRS straight, Turn 9-10 chicane exit. Historically 40-45 overtakes per race.
- **Key Features:** Multiple elevation changes, high-speed corners, technical middle sector rewards car balance
- **Historical Avg:** 42 overtakes per race (2022-2024 average)

### 2026 Regulation Impact
- Active aerodynamics replaces DRS — every car gets X-mode on straights regardless of gap, potentially increasing natural overtaking
- Energy management critical with 50/50 power split — teams with superior battery deployment may show late-race advantage
- MGU-K disabled for first 50m creates more volatile race starts and first-lap position changes
- Lighter, shorter cars with less downforce may change the traditional Albert Park overtaking zones

### Weather Forecast
**Current:** Partly cloudy, 20°C qualifying, 22°C race day. **Rain probability: 30%.** March in Melbourne is unpredictable — afternoon thunderstorms possible Sunday. Monitor radar Saturday evening.

## Key Storylines

### 1. Season Opener Data Vacuum
First race with zero historical 2026 performance data. Pre-season testing showed Mercedes dominance and Ferrari competitiveness, but race distance energy management remains untested. Fantasy implications: conservative picks with proven pre-season form trump speculative differential plays.

### 2. Leclerc's Testing Dominance Meets Reality
Charles Leclerc posted fastest testing times and shows strong FP2 long-run pace. Ferrari's pre-season looked genuine, not sandbagging. At 32% ownership and $22.8M, he's the consensus premium pick but not overdifferentiated. 2X Boost candidate with high ceiling if Ferrari delivers on testing promise.

### 3. Aston Martin Crisis Creates Value Traps
Honda engine reliability is confirmed problematic. Both Alonso ($10.0M) and Stroll ($8.0M) are planning early retirements. Despite low prices, they're DNF risks rather than value opportunities. Their sub-5% combined ownership suggests the market correctly identifies this risk.

### 4. Bearman vs Field Differential Opportunity
Oliver Bearman at $7.4M has 56% ownership — highest on the grid. Market consensus sees him as the safest value pick. However, Esteban Ocon (same team, $7.3M) has only 15% ownership despite superior 2025 PPM (24.25 vs Bearman's 27.84). Both benefit from Ferrari PU, but Ocon provides massive differential upside if he outscores Bearman.

### 5. Red Bull Second Seat Curse Continues
Isack Hadjar inherits the historically cursed Red Bull second seat at $15.1M (32% ownership). His 2025 PPM was only 9.85, yet he's priced in Tier B middle range. If Verstappen dominates and Hadjar struggles, Red Bull as a constructor ($28.2M) becomes a classic "split" team paying premium for one driver's performance.
